AllTrails Usability Study

← Back to Work

For a final class project, some fellow students and I conducted a usability study on the AllTrails app. AllTrails is a mobile and web app that helps outdoor enthusiasts discover, navigate, and review hiking, biking, and running trails.

The project identified key usability issues in the AllTrails app, and provided actionable recommendations to improve task completion and user experience.

Role

UX Researcher
Co-moderator for usability sessions
Note taker

Duration

3 weeks
(April 2025)

Team

Team of 4 MSU Students

Purpose & Goals

This usability study focused on the ease of use, learnability, and intuitiveness of the AllTrails app from the perspective of a user that is unfamiliar with the system.
The primary goals for this study were:

  • Establish how intuitive the filtering functionalities are for users seeking specific trail types
  • Assess how easily users can access important trail information
  • Evaluate the usability of the app's social features
  • Analyze the clarity and efficiency of the app's organizational features

Methodology

  • User Personas
  • Moderated Usability Study
  • Usability Aspect Reports

User Personas

The first step was to establish a couple user personas. We did this to give our team a better idea of the users for AllTrails.

Persona #1

Name: Joseph Gribble
Age: 21
Occupation: College Student
Location: East Lansing, MI

Background: Joseph is a junior at Michigan State studying computer science. He's passionate about technology and the outdoors. During the week he is busy with classes and homework, but would still like to make time for outdoor activities. Since he is a college student, he does not have much disposable income.

Goals: Discover new local trails to explore with friends, find trails that do not require entrance fees, check real-time trail conditions, and organize trails into lists for weekend plans or short breaks during the week.

Needs: Relevant trail recommendations, up-to-date trail information, efficient filter and search functions, progress tracking, low barrier to entry (free or affordable to use)

Persona #2

Name: Jeffrey Boomhauer
Age: 45
Occupation: Business Analyst
Location: Berkeley, CA

Background: Jeffrey is a senior business analyst at a mid-sized company. He lives with his wife, two children (10 and 14), and their energetic Australian Shepherd. He works full-time and is in front of a computer all day. To stay active, Jeffrey likes to walk his dog daily after work, and also explore trails with his family on the weekends.

Goals: Find nearby dog and kid-friendly trails, track personal and family progess, coordinate and plan hikes efficiently, and find out-of-state trails for family vacations.

Needs: Dog and kid-friendly trail filters, reliable trail information, efficient planning tools, ability to save and share trails, route previews and difficulty information, and a clean interface.

Moderated Usability Study

Methodology

  • 6 Participants - This number was chosen mostly because it was the required number for this assignment.
  • Convenience sampling - Due to the time constraints for this project, this was the only way we cold recruit participants. All participants were Michigan State students betwen 20-25 years old and had experience with mobile apps.
  • In-person and Zoom sessions - Every session included a moderator to facilitate the study and answer questions, as well as a note-taker to observe and record the actions, behaviors, and responses of participants.
  • Session recordings - Each session was documented using an audio recording, allowing for insights to be evidence based.

Task Descriptions

These tasks were chosen based off of user personas. We tried to emphasize tasks that would help the personas achieve their goals.

  • Find a dog-friendly trail and check the conditions
  • Locate Sleepy Hollow State Park and see if a pass or fee is required (without using the search function)
  • Mark a nearby trail as complete and leave a simple review
  • Create a new trail list and add an out of state trail

Results

Usability Aspect Reports

We tracked and analyzed critical usability issues observed during user testing. These findings helped us understand where users struggled and informed our recommendations for improvement. In total, we created 12 of these usability aspect reports. I have included a few of the most important critical isues below.

Issue: Inconsistent “Passes & Fees” Section

Observed During: Task 2

What Happened: Some participants couldn't locate the "Passes & Fees" section on specific trails. There was also an issue of fee amounts not being displayed. These inconsistencies led to confusion and task failure.

Why It Matters: Missing fee information could result in users being unprepared, leading to poor user trust or legal issues (e.g., tickets).

Recommendation: Always display the "Passes & Fees" section—even if no info is available—along with a note like "No fees required." Include the dollar amount of passes and fees to avoid unpreparedness.

“Maybe it only shows it if the trail actually requires a pass, I can't find this section anywhere in the trail information”
Issue: Add-to-List Button Missing or Hidden

Observed During: Task 4

What Happened: Users had difficulty adding a trail to a list because the “add” or “save” button was either missing or not discoverable on the trail page.

Why It Matters: Adding trails to lists is a core function of the app. If users can’t locate this button, it undermines the organizational features AllTrails promotes.

Recommendation: Ensure every trail page includes a clearly labeled and visible "Save to List" button near the top of the interface.

“The popular trails are added to the list, but I’m not sure how to add this one… I don’t know where the thingy is for this one.”
Issue: Trail Results Organization Confusing

Observed During: Task 1

What Happened: Participants were unsure whether trail results were ordered by distance, difficulty, or rating. This led to hesitation and second-guessing during the trail selection process.

Why It Matters: If users don’t understand how results are sorted, it adds cognitive load and can make the app feel unpredictable or disorganized.

Recommendation: Clarify how trail results are ordered—e.g., by default sort, distance, rating, or difficulty—using labels or tooltips above the result list.

“I got confused… is it based on how far away it is, or how long it is, or the star rating?”
Issue: Can't End a Custom Hike

Observed During: Task 4 (accidental)

What Happened: One user accidentally started a custom hike while trying to add a trail to a list and then couldn’t figure out how to stop the hike once they left the “Navigate” screen.

Why It Matters: Unintended activity tracking may result in inaccurate data, battery drain, or user frustration—especially if they don’t know the app is still running in the background.

Recommendation: Add a persistent mini-widget or indicator that allows users to pause/end a hike from any screen, or confirm start/stop more clearly within the Navigation UI.

“I’m stuck on the map. How do I get off the custom map now?”

Summary Table

In the table below, each participant was evaluated on a success/failure basis. A success is defined as completing the given task without requiring assistance, and a failure is defined as requiring additional help from the moderator in order to complete the given task.

Recommendations

Based on our usability study, we identified several actionable ways to improve consistency, feature accessibility, and user feedback within the AllTrails mobile app.

Usability Improvements

  • Unify page structure: Ensure all trail pages display standard sections like “Passes and Fees,” even when data is unavailable. Use placeholder text (such as “No fees listed for this trail.”).
  • Clarify sorting of trail lists: Show users how results are organized (e.g., distance, popularity) to reduce confusion.
  • Rename “Saved” tab to “Lists”: Makes the feature’s purpose clearer and easier to locate.
  • Make the “Save to List” button consistent: Ensure users can add trails from any trail page, not just from popular listings.
  • Improve interface consistency: Parks and trails should share a consistent layout and button options.
  • Rename buttons for clarity: For example, label the download button as “Download Map” to avoid confusion with saving or bookmarking.
  • Add an “End Hike” button: Provide an easy way to stop a hike once started. Consider a floating widget with “pause/end” functionality.
  • Enhance feedback indicators: Lengthen the duration of review submission confirmation popups to reassure users their action was successful.

Interface & Accessibility Enhancements

  • Highlight sorting logic visually: Bold or label what each trail list is sorted by (e.g., “Sorted by distance”).
  • Improve visibility of key buttons: Relocate or emphasize hard-to-find actions like “mark as complete.”
  • Use industry standards: Apply clear navigation, back options, and icon labels to meet user expectations.
  • Label trail actions clearly: For instance, users should not be able to leave a review without also marking a trail complete.
  • Address premium feature confusion: Make it more obvious which features are paid vs. free, especially around downloads.

Lessons Learned

What Worked Well

  • We developed detailed user personas early, which helped us create realistic, relevant tasks.
  • Running moderated sessions allowed us to observe subtle user frustrations in real-time.
  • Working as a team of four allowed us to divide tasks efficiently: moderation, note-taking, and analysis.
  • We gathered useful qualitative insights that led to actionable recommendations without needing to build a prototype.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Recruit more than 6 participants, which would allow for more insight into how effective the current app is.
  • Recruit a more diverse range of participants to avoid over-relying on a college-student demographic.
  • Visualize insights with mockups to support recommendations.
  • Add more tasks for users to complete, as there were some parts of the app we did not gert to test.
  • Record more than two critical incidents per participant.

Next Steps

While this was completed for a class, if it were to be an actual commercial project these are some of the next steps I would take:

  • Develop low-fidelity wireframes to explore solutions for the most critical usability issues, such as saving trails or organizing trail lists.
  • Conduct follow-up testing using updated tasks and proposed interface changes to validate whether the recommendations improve performance.
  • Expand participant demographics beyond college students to include parents, older adults, and casual outdoor users for broader insight.
  • Collaborate with designers to create polished UI mockups that address both visual clarity and accessibility best practices.
  • Present findings to stakeholders to make the case for design changes and begin the process of actually implementing the ideas within the app.

Signal Messenger Heuristic Evaluation and Cognitive Walkthrough

← Back to Work

This study was done for a class project where some fellow students and I conducted a heuristic evaluation and cognitive walkthrough on the Signal Messenger app.

Signal is a free, open-source messaging app focused on privacy and end-to-end encryption. It allows users to send texts and voice messages, as well as secure calls without collecting or storing personal data.

Role

UX Researcher

Duration

2 weeks
(March 2025)

Team

Team of 4 MSU Students

Purpose & Goals

This evaluation focused on the usability, consistency, and learnability of the Signal Messenger app. Using a combination of heuristic evaluation and cognitive walkthroughs, our goal was to uncover usability issues that affect the onboarding experience, contact management, privacy controls, and core communication features such as messaging and calling.

  • Evaluate how intuitive Signal’s interface is for first-time users during common tasks like adding contacts or verifying privacy settings
  • Assess whether Signal's UI adheres to standard design heuristics
  • Identify points of friction or confusion that could disrupt core user actions like calling, messaging, or editing settings
  • Generate actionable recommendations that improve usability while preserving the privacy of users

Methodology

  • Heuristic Evaluation: We reviewed Signal using Jakob Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics, providing severity ratings to issues based on frequency, impact on functionality, and persistence
  • Cognitive Walkthrough: We simulated user flows for two common tasks: verifying authenticity of contacts, and adding a new contact through "New Messages." These tasks were then evaluated from the perspective of a new user.

Heuristic Evaluation

We began this section with every member of our group completing their own seperate evaluations, and then we consolidated our evaluations into one document. Combined, our team came up with 23 heuristic violations; however, for the sake of this portfolio I will only include violations that I personally found. Note that these are not all of the heuristics I found, and the full report will be linked at the bottom of this page.

User Control and Freedom

Severity: 2

Location: "Who can find you with your number" page

How Violated: Only gives the user an option to have either everybody or nobody find you with your number without an in between option.

Recommendation: Allow users to select only certain contacts to be able to find the user with their number.

Recognition Rather Than Recall

Severity: 2

Location: "Chats" page

How Violated: In order to filter your messages to see which ones are unread, the user has to either click on their profile in the top left corner or swipe up on the existing chats. Neither of these are very intuitive, and I thought the swipe up would simply refresh the page.

Recommendation: Add a filter button that allows for the user to filter through their messages in various ways, not just through filtering by unread.

Error Prevention

Severity: 3

Location: “Notifications” section of settings

How Violated: When a user clicks on “Re-register Push Notifications” it simply just tells the user that they have successfully re-registered for push notifications without providing further explanation.

Recommendation: Have this option use a button to re-register for push notifications and include a description of what exactly this does so the user knows if they want to or not. Also include the option to undo this.

Help and Documentation

Severity: 2

Location: Advanced privacy settings

How Violated: Not every advanced privacy feature is explained, there is even a “Censorship Circumnavigation” button that you cannot turn on because “you are already connected to the Signal service” however, it does not explain this in detail.

Recommendation: Have documentation for all of the advanced privacy features, this is only present in the “Sealed Sender” section

Cognitive Walkthroughs

A cognitive walkthough is a usability evaluation method that assesses how easily a new user can complete can complete specific tasks within an interface. We used this method to identify points of confusion or friction in Signal's core workflows, focusing on whether first-time users could navigate features without prior knowledge or external help. This allowed us to evaluate the app’s learnability and surface-level intuitiveness, especially for functions related to adding new contacts.

Note: Successful steps will be green, and unsuccessful steps will be red.

Task 1: Verify Authenticity of Contacts

Objective: Evaluate if a first-time user can successfully verify a contact’s safety number to confirm secure end-to-end encryption.

Step 1: Navigate to Contact List
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes, by opening the app the user will be presented with the correct page.
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? Yes, the contact/messaging page shows users a list of people they’ve spoken with or called on the app.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? Yes, they will see a list of people they can find safety numbers for by navigating to this page.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? Most likely, aside from having a separate “Contacts” page, the “Chats” page does a good job of showing users the individuals they can find safety numbers for.
Step 2: Click on a Direct Message
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes.
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? Yes, in most messaging apps, clicking on a person's name will open the messages with them, so it is intuitive.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? Yes, if the user is attempting to open a direct message, the user will click on the direct message.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? Yes, if the direct message is clicked on, the chat will expand and show all messages, communicating to the user that the action leads to the desired result.
Step 3: Find Contact Information
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes.
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? No, it is not clear that clicking on the person's name once you are within a direct message will expand more details.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? No, they would most likely discover this feature on accident, because there is no icon or button that would imply that more options are available.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? Yes, if the user clicks on the name in the direct message, they will notice that another page will open up, with more options available.
Step 4: Navigate to Safety Number
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes, by opening the app the user will be presented with the correct page.
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? Yes, the contact/messaging page shows users a list of people they’ve spoken with or called on the app.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? Yes, they will see a list of people they can find safety numbers for by navigating to this page.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? Most likely, aside from having a separate “Contacts” page, the “Chats” page does a good job of showing users the individuals they can find safety numbers for.

Insight: While most of the task flow is intuitive to new users, we found there was an issue with tapping a contact’s name within a direct message to show contact information. While I think this may be intuitive to some users that more information can be found by clicking on the name, in the context of trying to verify a safety number it can be confusing. Improving the visibility of this feature, through an icon that shows up the first time opening a message for a new user or a more conventional menu would make this critical security feature more discoverable and easier to complete.

Task 2: Add a new contact through "New Messages"

Objective: Evaluate if a first-time user can successfully add a new contact through the "New Messages" screen.

Step 1: Navigate to "New Messages"
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes, the symbol used to open the “new message” prompt is generally used across platforms to create a draft.
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? Yes, it is visible and clear.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? Yes, the page is titled correctly.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? Yes.
Step 2: Click on a Direct Message
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes, it is immediately visible at the top of the “new message” window in a text box related to “contacts.”
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? Yes, it is properly titled and clearly interactable.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? No, it is not generally surmisable that this will relocate to a window to add new contacts in the app.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? Yes, a new page is opened that directs them to the next step.
Step 3: Find Desired Contact Using the Search Bar
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? No, the search bar freezes when in use.
  • We did not need to assess this step more, as it already produced a critical error
Step 4: Select and Add New Contact
  • 1. Will the users try to achieve the right effect? Yes, if not using the search bar. The list is clear and interactable.
  • 2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? Yes, the buttons are observably interactable.
  • 3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect they are trying to achieve? Yes.
  • 4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see that progress is being made toward the goal of the task? No, once clicking done there is no indication that the user’s action did anything or confirmation that the contacts were added. The window just closes.

Insight: While the task of adding a new contact through “New Messages” begins with a familiar and clearly labeled entry point, users encounter several usability issues as they progress. The pathway to add a contact is not intuitive, as users may not realize that clicking into the contact input field opens a new screen for contact management, due to a lack of visual cues or guidance. Additionally, the search functionality within that screen is unreliable, freezing during use and preventing users from locating contacts. Finally, after selecting a contact, the app offers no confirmation or feedback that the action was successful, leaving users unsure if the contact was added. These issues highlight gaps in discoverability, error prevention, and visibility of system status, which collectively hinder task completion and user confidence.

Recommendations

Based on our cognitive walkthrough and heuristic evaluation of the Signal app, we identified a range of usability challenges related to feature discoverability, interface consistency, and system feedback. These issues impact task success, particularly when users attempt to add new contacts or receive calls. The following recommendations aim to improve task flow clarity, interface predictability, and user confidence in completing core actions within the app.

Usability Improvements

  • Fix search bar freezing: Ensure the search bar in the “New Messages” screen functions properly to prevent task failure when searching for a contact.
  • Add feedback for contact addition: After clicking “Done,” provide a confirmation popup or visual indication that a contact was successfully added.
  • Introduce direct contact add option: Allow users to add new contacts from the app settings or a dedicated “Contacts” page without needing to initiate a message.
  • Clarify action pathways: Update the labeling and behavior of the “contacts” field in the “New Messages” screen to clearly indicate that it can be used to add new contacts.

Interface & Accessibility Enhancements

  • Improve system feedback: Provide clearer and more consistent visual feedback when user actions are completed (e.g., adding contacts, sending messages).
  • Standardize navigation: Unify the behavior of profile and navigation icons across all major app sections to avoid confusion and unexpected menu changes.
  • Enhance error prevention: Clarify which features require acceptance (e.g., message requests) and ensure that users in contact lists do not face unnecessary barriers to communication.
  • Add tutorial for new users: Incorporate a brief onboarding or guided tutorial to explain key features like adding contacts, starting calls, and navigating safety settings.

Lessons Learned

What Worked Well

  • Using Nielsen’s heuristics gave us a structured and repeatable framework to evaluate usability issues.
  • The cognitive walkthrough allowed us to view the app through the lens of a first-time user and uncover gaps in learnability.
  • Breaking down tasks step-by-step made it easier to pinpoint where confusion or friction occurred in the user journey.
  • Our recommendations directly tied to observed usability breakdowns, which helped us form clear, actionable suggestions.
  • Each team member doing individual heuristic evaluations allowed for more discoveries and put emphasis on repeated violations.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Include more user perspectives through usability testing to validate the issues found in heuristic and walkthrough evaluations.
  • Explore visual design solutions such as wireframes to better communicate how recommended changes might appear in-app.
  • Spend more time exploring accessibility elements like text labels, icon contrast, and voiceover compatibility.
  • Conduct walkthroughs on both iOS and Android to compare consistency and user experience across platforms.

Next Steps

While this project was completed for a class, if it were to continue in a professional setting, these are the next steps I would pursue:

  • Create annotated wireframes to visually demonstrate improvements to discoverability and user feedback for contact management flows.
  • Prototype interaction fixes such as improved button labels and confirmation messages to test usability before implementation.
  • Conduct usability tests with a broader range of users to validate that updates address the key friction points identified.
  • Meet with development and design teams to review technical feasibility and refine designs based on platform guidelines.
  • Audit accessibility for issues like contrast, icon labeling, and screen reader support to ensure inclusive design across devices.

Mental Health App Competitive Analysis & Concept

← Back to Work

This project was part of an individual UX research assignment where I analyzed three mental health chatbot apps: Woebot, Mello, and Wysa to identify design strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities.

The goal was to develop a conceptual direction for a user-friendly and privacy-focused mental health app.

Role

UX Researcher
Concept Strategist
UI Designer

Duration

2 weeks
(October-November 2024)

Team

Me, myself, and I

Tools Used

Figma
Pencil & Paper

Purpose & Goals

This competitive analysis and concept exploration were conducted to better understand how existing mental health apps support user well-being and identify opportunities for improvement. The goal was to inform the design of a new mental health mobile experience aimed at incorporating AI assistance and maximum privacy.

The primary goals of this project were:

  • Analyze strengths and weaknesses of current mental health apps such as Calm, Headspace, and Woebot
  • Identify gaps in features and usability for mental health solutions
  • Design a concept that emphasizes simplicity, privacy, and an AI element
  • Establish core functionalities that promote mindfulness, journaling, and check-ins without giving up personal information to the app

Methodology

  • Competitive Analysis
  • Sketching
  • Wireframing

Competitive Analysis

To understand the current landscape of mental health apps with an AI feature, I analyzed three popular platforms Mello, Wysa, and Woebot. I made sure to focus on functionality, design, accessibility, and emotional tone. This analysis helped highlight both best practices and opportunities for improvement in my own concept.

Mello

Strengths:

  • Chatbot integrates with the tools section to make tracking progress easy
  • Lots of multimedia on the home page to engage the user
  • Personalized experience, personality tests and mood determine how the home page looks

Weaknesses:

  • Data privacy concerns, the app requires you to sign lengthy terms of service agreements, and you need an account to use
  • Subscription cost, there is a weekly subscription fee of $8 which is more expensive than most streaming services
  • Mood tracking doesn’t give much information on long term trends, mostly just has information on recent mood tracking

Notable Feature:

Calming prompts and daily encouragement messages

Wysa

Strengths:

  • Clean and intuitive interface, things are easy to find and understand
  • anonymous, users don’t make an account making privacy not much of a concern
  • Offers human interaction with the “coach” feature

Weaknesses:

  • The paywall for the coaching section is annoying, if you accidentally click on “coach” or would like more information, it immediately sends you to a paywall
  • Chatbot is hard to find, it is on the home page in the “self-care” section but not on the “self- care” page
  • Free options are reliant on self-guidance and self-motivation which can be difficult for certain users with depression or anxiety

Notable Feature:

Option to escalate to a real therapist or coach

Woebot

Strengths:

  • Simple interface makes navigation and understanding easy
  • Chatbot integrates with the tools section to make tracking progress easy
  • 24/7 availability of mental health help, it doesn’t fully replace human interaction but can help whenever

Weaknesses:

  • Limited personalization for users
  • Responses from the bot can feel generic, there is not an option for human interaction
  • Limited visual engagement, pretty much everything is text based with only a small amount of multimedia

Notable Feature:

Conversation-based check-ins and emotional tracking

Key Takeaways

  • There is a strong user need for personalization, simplicity, and emotional support.
  • Many top apps gate essential features behind a paywall, limiting accessibility.
  • A calm and minimal interface is more effective for stressed or anxious users.
  • Chatbot-based approaches (like Woebot) are fine for an always available solution, but there needs to also be some way to connect users to another human.

Low Fidelity Sketches

Before moving into a wireframe, I began by sketching core screens to explore the layout and flow of the mental health app. These low-fidelity drawings helped shape the different pages of the app, such as a loading screen, chatbot, planner, home/welcome screen, and resources/lessons.

Key Features:

  • Home Page: Mood tracker, lesson plan, resources, and schedule all accessible.
  • Chatbot: Simple interface with "history" to view all chat history and "summary" that gives a quick overview of the current conversation to emphasize talking points and aid reflection.
  • Journal: Opens on a schedule that allows users to track each day and retroactively add journal entries. Once user clicks on a day, they can type in their thoughts. There is a sidebar menu that opens allowing for users to add items to their schedule as they are journaling.
  • Resources and Lessons: This page has resources for users to learn about mental health and helpful strategies (Lessons), get connected with local providers (Providers), track their mood and emotions (Mood Tracker), and connect with other people in the mental health community by asking questions or starting conversations (Forums).

Slightly Higher Fidelity Figma Wireframe

After sketching out early concepts, I created a low-fidelity digital wireframe in Figma to begin refining layout, flow, and tone.

Note: Click on the "Home" button on the nav bar to be directed to the welcome page. No matter what I do it seems to want to start on the chatbot!

Lessons Learned

What Worked Well

  • Reviewing multiple mental health apps gave valuable insight into what should be included in my own designs.
  • Reviewing other apps also gave me ideas for how to improve upon what is already in the market.
  • Sketching first helped clarify core features before investing time into digital wireframes.
  • The Figma prototype allowed for quick visual iteration and to see if my sketched ideas translate into an aesthetically pleasing app.
  • Breaking down competitor strengths and weaknesses sharpened the goals of my own concept.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Test early concepts with real users to validate whether features meet needs and make sense.
  • Produce paper prototypes to discover design flaws before committing to digital mockups.
  • Include expert feedback (e.g., therapists or mental health professionals) to flesh out ideas.
  • Create a higher fidelity prototype with custom images and designs to help with user testing.

Next Steps

While this was completed for a class, if it were to be an actual commercial project these are some of the next steps I would take:

  • Conduct user interviews with individuals navigating stress or emotional challenges to validate key features and emotional tone.
  • Develop high-fidelity mockups that incorporate calming visual language, accessibility best practices, and consistent interaction design.
  • Expand the Figma prototype to include full user flows like onboarding, journaling, and chatbot conversations for usability testing.
  • Perform usability testing with a diverse group of users to identify interaction breakdowns and emotional engagement effectiveness.
  • Consult with mental health professionals to refine content tone and ensure ethical and supportive design choices.
