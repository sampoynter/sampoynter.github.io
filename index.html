AllTrails Usability Study

‚Üê Back to Work

For a final class project, some fellow students and I conducted a usability study on the AllTrails app. AllTrails is a mobile and web app that helps outdoor enthusiasts discover, navigate, and review hiking, biking, and running trails.

The project identified key usability issues in the AllTrails app, and provided actionable recommendations to improve task completion and user experience.

Role

UX Researcher
Co-moderator for usability sessions
Note taker

Duration

3 weeks
(April 2025)

Team

Team of 4 MSU Students

Purpose & Goals

This usability study focused on the ease of use, learnability, and intuitiveness of the AllTrails app from the perspective of a user that is unfamiliar with the system.
The primary goals for this study were:

  • Establish how intuitive the filtering functionalities are for users seeking specific trail types
  • Assess how easily users can access important trail information
  • Evaluate the usability of the app's social features
  • Analyze the clarity and efficiency of the app's organizational features

Methodology

  • User Personas
  • Moderated Usability Study
  • Usability Aspect Reports

User Personas

The first step was to establish a couple user personas. We did this to give our team a better idea of the users for AllTrails.

Persona #1

Name: Joseph Gribble
Age: 21
Occupation: College Student
Location: East Lansing, MI

Background: Joseph is a junior at Michigan State studying computer science. He's passionate about technology and the outdoors. During the week he is busy with classes and homework, but would still like to make time for outdoor activities. Since he is a college student, he does not have much disposable income.

Goals: Discover new local trails to explore with friends, find trails that do not require entrance fees, check real-time trail conditions, and organize trails into lists for weekend plans or short breaks during the week.

Needs: Relevant trail recommendations, up-to-date trail information, efficient filter and search functions, progress tracking, low barrier to entry (free or affordable to use)

Persona #2

Name: Jeffrey Boomhauer
Age: 45
Occupation: Business Analyst
Location: Berkeley, CA

Background: Jeffrey is a senior business analyst at a mid-sized company. He lives with his wife, two children (10 and 14), and their energetic Australian Shepherd. He works full-time and is in front of a computer all day. To stay active, Jeffrey likes to walk his dog daily after work, and also explore trails with his family on the weekends.

Goals: Find nearby dog and kid-friendly trails, track personal and family progess, coordinate and plan hikes efficiently, and find out-of-state trails for family vacations.

Needs: Dog and kid-friendly trail filters, reliable trail information, efficient planning tools, ability to save and share trails, route previews and difficulty information, and a clean interface.

Moderated Usability Study

Methodology

  • 6 Participants - This number was chosen mostly because it was the required number for this assignment.
  • Convenience sampling - Due to the time constraints for this project, this was the only way we cold recruit participants. All participants were Michigan State students betwen 20-25 years old and had experience with mobile apps.
  • In-person and Zoom sessions - Every session included a moderator to facilitate the study and answer questions, as well as a note-taker to observe and record the actions, behaviors, and responses of participants.
  • Session recordings - Each session was documented using an audio recording, allowing for insights to be evidence based.

Task Descriptions

These tasks were chosen based off of user personas. We tried to emphasize tasks that would help the personas achieve their goals.

  • Find a dog-friendly trail and check the conditions
  • Locate Sleepy Hollow State Park and see if a pass or fee is required (without using the search function)
  • Mark a nearby trail as complete and leave a simple review
  • Create a new trail list and add an out of state trail

Results

Usability Aspect Reports

We tracked and analyzed critical usability issues observed during user testing. These findings helped us understand where users struggled and informed our recommendations for improvement. In total, we created 12 of these usability aspect reports. I have included a few of the most important critical isues below.

Issue: Inconsistent ‚ÄúPasses & Fees‚Äù Section

Observed During: Task 2

What Happened: Some participants couldn't locate the "Passes & Fees" section on specific trails. There was also an issue of fee amounts not being displayed. These inconsistencies led to confusion and task failure.

Why It Matters: Missing fee information could result in users being unprepared, leading to poor user trust or legal issues (e.g., tickets).

Recommendation: Always display the "Passes & Fees" section‚Äîeven if no info is available‚Äîalong with a note like "No fees required." Include the dollar amount of passes and fees to avoid unpreparedness.

‚ÄúMaybe it only shows it if the trail actually requires a pass, I can't find this section anywhere in the trail information‚Äù
Issue: Add-to-List Button Missing or Hidden

Observed During: Task 4

What Happened: Users had difficulty adding a trail to a list because the ‚Äúadd‚Äù or ‚Äúsave‚Äù button was either missing or not discoverable on the trail page.

Why It Matters: Adding trails to lists is a core function of the app. If users can‚Äôt locate this button, it undermines the organizational features AllTrails promotes.

Recommendation: Ensure every trail page includes a clearly labeled and visible "Save to List" button near the top of the interface.

‚ÄúThe popular trails are added to the list, but I‚Äôm not sure how to add this one‚Ä¶ I don‚Äôt know where the thingy is for this one.‚Äù
Issue: Trail Results Organization Confusing

Observed During: Task 1

What Happened: Participants were unsure whether trail results were ordered by distance, difficulty, or rating. This led to hesitation and second-guessing during the trail selection process.

Why It Matters: If users don‚Äôt understand how results are sorted, it adds cognitive load and can make the app feel unpredictable or disorganized.

Recommendation: Clarify how trail results are ordered‚Äîe.g., by default sort, distance, rating, or difficulty‚Äîusing labels or tooltips above the result list.

‚ÄúI got confused‚Ä¶ is it based on how far away it is, or how long it is, or the star rating?‚Äù
Issue: Can't End a Custom Hike

Observed During: Task 4 (accidental)

What Happened: One user accidentally started a custom hike while trying to add a trail to a list and then couldn‚Äôt figure out how to stop the hike once they left the ‚ÄúNavigate‚Äù screen.

Why It Matters: Unintended activity tracking may result in inaccurate data, battery drain, or user frustration‚Äîespecially if they don‚Äôt know the app is still running in the background.

Recommendation: Add a persistent mini-widget or indicator that allows users to pause/end a hike from any screen, or confirm start/stop more clearly within the Navigation UI.

‚ÄúI‚Äôm stuck on the map. How do I get off the custom map now?‚Äù

Recommendations

Based on our usability study, we identified several actionable ways to improve consistency, feature accessibility, and user feedback within the AllTrails mobile app.

Usability Improvements

  • Unify page structure: Ensure all trail pages display standard sections like ‚ÄúPasses and Fees,‚Äù even when data is unavailable. Use placeholder text (such as ‚ÄúNo fees listed for this trail.‚Äù).
  • Clarify sorting of trail lists: Show users how results are organized (e.g., distance, popularity) to reduce confusion.
  • Rename ‚ÄúSaved‚Äù tab to ‚ÄúLists‚Äù: Makes the feature‚Äôs purpose clearer and easier to locate.
  • Make the ‚ÄúSave to List‚Äù button consistent: Ensure users can add trails from any trail page, not just from popular listings.
  • Improve interface consistency: Parks and trails should share a consistent layout and button options.
  • Rename buttons for clarity: For example, label the download button as ‚ÄúDownload Map‚Äù to avoid confusion with saving or bookmarking.
  • Add an ‚ÄúEnd Hike‚Äù button: Provide an easy way to stop a hike once started. Consider a floating widget with ‚Äúpause/end‚Äù functionality.
  • Enhance feedback indicators: Lengthen the duration of review submission confirmation popups to reassure users their action was successful.

Interface & Accessibility Enhancements

  • Highlight sorting logic visually: Bold or label what each trail list is sorted by (e.g., ‚ÄúSorted by distance‚Äù).
  • Improve visibility of key buttons: Relocate or emphasize hard-to-find actions like ‚Äúmark as complete.‚Äù
  • Use industry standards: Apply clear navigation, back options, and icon labels to meet user expectations.
  • Label trail actions clearly: For instance, users should not be able to leave a review without also marking a trail complete.
  • Address premium feature confusion: Make it more obvious which features are paid vs. free, especially around downloads.

Lessons Learned

What Worked Well

  • We developed detailed user personas early, which helped us create realistic, relevant tasks.
  • Running moderated sessions allowed us to observe subtle user frustrations in real-time.
  • Working as a team of four allowed us to divide tasks efficiently: moderation, note-taking, and analysis.
  • We gathered useful qualitative insights that led to actionable recommendations without needing to build a prototype.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Recruit more than 6 participants, which would allow for more insight into how effective the current app is.
  • Recruit a more diverse range of participants to avoid over-relying on a college-student demographic.
  • Visualize insights with mockups to support recommendations.
  • Add more tasks for users to complete, as there were some parts of the app we did not gert to test.
  • Record more than two critical incidents per participant.

Next Steps

While this was completed for a class, if it were to be an actual commercial project these are some of the next steps I would take:

  • Develop low-fidelity wireframes to explore solutions for the most critical usability issues, such as saving trails or organizing trail lists.
  • Conduct follow-up testing using updated tasks and proposed interface changes to validate whether the recommendations improve performance.
  • Expand participant demographics beyond college students to include parents, older adults, and casual outdoor users for broader insight.
  • Collaborate with designers to create polished UI mockups that address both visual clarity and accessibility best practices.
  • Present findings to stakeholders to make the case for design changes and begin the process of actually implementing the ideas within the app.

Signal Messenger Heuristic Evaluation and Cognitive Walkthrough

‚Üê Back to Work

This study was done for a class project where some fellow students and I conducted a heuristic evaluation and cognitive walkthrough on the Signal Messenger app.

Signal is a free, open-source messaging app focused on privacy and end-to-end encryption. It allows users to send texts and voice messages, as well as secure calls without collecting or storing personal data.

Role

UX Researcher

Duration

2 weeks
(March 2025)

Team

Team of 4 MSU Students

Purpose & Goals

This evaluation focused on the usability, consistency, and learnability of the Signal Messenger app. Using a combination of heuristic evaluation and cognitive walkthroughs, our goal was to uncover usability issues that affect the onboarding experience, contact management, privacy controls, and core communication features such as messaging and calling.

  • Evaluate how intuitive Signal‚Äôs interface is for first-time users during common tasks like adding contacts or verifying privacy settings
  • Assess whether Signal's UI adheres to standard design heuristics
  • Identify points of friction or confusion that could disrupt core user actions like calling, messaging, or editing settings
  • Generate actionable recommendations that improve usability while preserving the privacy of users

Methodology

  • Heuristic Evaluation: We reviewed Signal using Jakob Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics, providing severity ratings to issues based on frequency, impact on functionality, and persistence
  • Cognitive Walkthrough: We simulated user flows for two common tasks: verifying authenticity of contacts, and adding a new contact through "New Messages." These tasks were then evaluated from the perspective of a new user.

Heuristic Evaluation

We began this section with every member of our group completing their own seperate evaluations, and then we consolidated our evaluations into one document. Combined, our team came up with 23 heuristic violations; however, for the sake of this portfolio I will only include violations that I personally found. Note that these are not all of the heuristics I found, and the full report will be linked at the bottom of this page.

User Control and Freedom

Severity: 2

Location: "Who can find you with your number" page

How Violated: Only gives the user an option to have either everybody or nobody find you with your number without an in between option.

Recommendation: Allow users to select only certain contacts to be able to find the user with their number.

Recognition Rather Than Recall

Severity: 2

Location: "Chats" page

How Violated: In order to filter your messages to see which ones are unread, the user has to either click on their profile in the top left corner or swipe up on the existing chats. Neither of these are very intuitive, and I thought the swipe up would simply refresh the page.

Recommendation: Add a filter button that allows for the user to filter through their messages in various ways, not just through filtering by unread.

Error Prevention

Severity: 3

Location: ‚ÄúNotifications‚Äù section of settings

How Violated: When a user clicks on ‚ÄúRe-register Push Notifications‚Äù it simply just tells the user that they have successfully re-registered for push notifications without providing further explanation.

Recommendation: Have this option use a button to re-register for push notifications and include a description of what exactly this does so the user knows if they want to or not. Also include the option to undo this.

Help and Documentation

Severity: 2

Location: Advanced privacy settings

How Violated: Not every advanced privacy feature is explained, there is even a ‚ÄúCensorship Circumnavigation‚Äù button that you cannot turn on because ‚Äúyou are already connected to the Signal service‚Äù however, it does not explain this in detail.

Recommendation: Have documentation for all of the advanced privacy features, this is only present in the ‚ÄúSealed Sender‚Äù section
